Wolverine Ticket Transfer

Buy Blue. Go Blue.

Context

Class: ENTR 390

Timeline: 4 weeks

Role: Researcher, Designer

Tools used: Balsamiq, Figma, Adobe Photoshop

Skills: UX Research, Personas, Empathy Mapping, Wireframing, Prototyping

The Problem

On a fall saturday unlike any other, I found myself in an extremely stressful situation - I didn't have a ticket to tonight's Michigan football game. I had previously sold my ticket to my friend as I didn't think I was going to be able to go, but at the last moment, my schedule cleared up. After scouring Facebook and getting scammed by a fake user I came to the conclusion:

Finding a ticket to Michigan football games as a Michigan student takes a lot of time, effort, and energy from the buyer and seller perspective.

Audience/Personas

In order to determine who my audience was, I brainstormed potential users of the product based on who would be able to relate to my problem statement. I brainstormed the following potential users of my application.

  • Michigan students who like to sell their tickets
  • Michigan students who want to bring friends to the games
  • Michigan students who donâ€™t know how to find student tickets
  • Michigan students who need tickets at the last minute and need a convenient way to find tickets

From there, I assigned my primary and 2 secondary personas. I thought that the buyer should be the primary persona because I believe the customer should always be at the heart of the product. I then assigned my secondary personas to be sellers who are selling their tickets for different reasons.

For my primary persona, I created an empathy map to really ensure that I was understanding the pain points.



I then used this empathy map to help me with my primary persona, and then created my two secondary personas. Emily Morris represents the primary persona and the "buyer" archetype.
Rashida Peterson and Josh Henderson represent secondary personas and the "seller" archetypes.



Design Process

After conducting my research, I settled on three outcomes I wanted my application to have. These included:

  • Reduce scams/fake ticket transfers, allow reporting and blocking of dishonest users
  • Allow users to easily find tickets to all games that they want to go to
  • Create platform where users can easily keep track of all ticket related conversations


Therefore, I knew my app needed the following key functions:

  • A home screen where you can easily find tickets
  • The ability to report and block dishonest users
  • A way to message other users safely and securely to conduct transations
  • Allow users to post tickets to sell

Iteration

After further iteration, I met with my instructors and my peers to discuss some two key questions that I had:

  • Does my user flow make sense?
  • Are the elements of the application readable?

I ended up making two critical changes based on this feedback.

Change 1: Showing the current page

Old version

New version



Previously, my application did not display the current page if you opened the menu from the bottom. This was a concern as it could potentially throw off the flow of the user and it does not provide the user with enough knowledge when they need to navigate to another page. I added a stroke around the current page in the menu so that users could use this as a visual indication of where they are in the application at the moment.

Change 2: Reformating the tickets screen

Old version

New version



My old tickets page utilized a small arrow to show whether you were selling or buying a ticket. Not only was this feature not clear to users (they did not know what the arrows meant), but it was an accessibility issue as it was not very large and could've been hard to see by users with worse vision. To mitigate this, I created two explicit labels that clearly defined what tickets users were buying and selling. I also seperated the two groups of tickets from each other. I decided to keep the arrows on the tickets to reinforce the idea that the ticket was being sold and going "outward" or being bought and coming "inward".

Rationale

I believe that I have created the best solution to my problem that finding and selling a Michigan football ticket requires a lot of effort from the buyer and seller perspective. This is because my solution makes it faster to find the ticket while the user completes the transation through a third party.

I was asked many times - wouldn't this application be better if you could just pay for the ticket in the application? My answer is a resounding no.

The goal of my application was to completely streamline the process of finding tickets to Michigan Football games, as the current solutions that exist include combing through various groupchats and GroupMes, and selling tickets on less-than-trustworthy websites. Websites where you can sell your tickets are not used by many people because people want credibility with services that handle financial transations - credibility that would be hard to establish in a brand new application. I wanted to create an application that users could trust, and this meant NOT handling financial data and instead simply providing a platform for users to find the tickets and the sellers/buyers they want. The ability to block and report users also ensure that this trust is kept while using the application.

I was influenced by a number of different ticket applications including stubhub, seatgeek, and ticketmaster when I was creating this application. As established ticket selling giants, their user interfaces were inspirations to the way I displayed by tickets to my users. In addition, I used Apple fonts such as SF Pro and SF Pro Compact throughout the application because I felt that they gave a very polished look to the application that I was missing when I was using other fonts.

User Feedback

After receiving feedback from my instructors and peers, I can say with confidence that I achieved my goal of creating a less painful and time-consuming way to find tickets to Michigan football games. Although there were a few errors and improvements I could make to the interface, most people that I asked really resonated with the problem and saw this solution as one that addressed it well. As mentioned, a lot of people expected to be able to pay for their tickets directly on the application. After explaining why I excluded the feature, they understood.

Many people mentioned that my application essentially abstracted out the search process when looking for tickets and got them straight to the bartering/messaging phase. This was a great comment to hear as it was what I believed to be the biggest bottleneck in the ticket process.

Next Steps

My instructors were able to provide me with great feedback regarding what to improve about my application. They mentioned inconsistencies with the design language in my application (i.e "View Tickets" tab led to the home screen) and pointed out that the hamburger menu I use implies that the menu will swipe in from the right, when in reality, it comes from the bottom. They also mentioned areas where the readability of my application could have been improved by increasing font sizes and whitespace.

I made sure to take these lessons with me into my next project in ENTR 390, and I hope to do even better on that project.

In terms of expansion, I would love to add features to use this application to buy basketball and hockey tickets. These tickets are also transferrable and sought after, and therefore require work to find if you do not have season tickets.
